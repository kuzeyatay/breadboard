## The Nature of Light

### A Long Time Ago in a Galaxy Far, Far Away...

- In **Plato's time (427-347 B.C.)**, reflection of light from smooth surfaces was known.
- **Aristotle (384-322 B.C.)**, ancient Greek philosopher, thought that we saw the world by sending "something" from our eye, which reflected from the object.
- The **ancient Greeks (around 200 A.D.)** also first observed the refraction of light, which occurs at the boundary of two transparent media with different refractive indices.

---

## Particle or Wave?

In the **17th century**, two scientists had different views about the nature of light:

- **Isaac Newton (1643-1727):** Light is **particles**
- **Christiaan Huygens (1629-1695):** Light is **waves**

---

## The Nature of Light: Particles or Waves?

- **Rømer** (Danish astronomer, 1676) measured the speed of light by observing the eclipse of Jupiter's moon Io:
  **c = 2.1 * 10^8 m/s**
- **Frizeau** (French scientist, 1849) measured:
  **c = 3.15 * 10^8 m/s**
- **James Clerk Maxwell (1873)** predicted electromagnetic waves travelling at approximately the speed of light.
- **Speed of light in vacuum:**
  **c = 299,792,458 m/s**
- **Heinrich Hertz (1887)** experimentally demonstrated the existence of electromagnetic waves other than light.

---

## The Nature of Light: Particles or Waves?

- **Albert Einstein (1905)** defined light particles (**photons**) to explain the photoelectric effect.
- **Quantum mechanics (around 1930):**
  - Light is both a **particle** and a **wave**
  - **Absorption and emission** of light are best described by particles
  - **Propagation** of light is best described by waves

## Wave Fronts and Light Rays

### Wave Fronts

- A **wave front** is a "leading edge of a wave".
- More formal definition:
  The locus of all adjacent points at which the phase of vibration of a physical quantity associated with the wave is the same.

### Rays

- A **ray** indicates the local direction of propagation of a wave front.
- In a **homogeneous isotropic medium**, rays are straight lines and are perpendicular to the wave front.
- The ray description of light is called **geometrical optics**.

### Rays - Plane Waves

- For a far-away source, the radii of the spherical wave fronts have become very large.
- A section of the spherical surface can be considered a plane.
- This is called a **plane wave**.

---

## Quiz: Wave Fronts

Can two wave fronts from the same wave cross one another?

- (a) **Yes**
- (b) **No** <- !!

Each wave front represents a certain phase. At a crossing, the wave front would have two distinct phases, which is not possible.

## Reflection and Refraction

### Introduction and Definition

At an interface between two materials, a wave is partly **reflected** and partly **refracted** (transmitted).

### Schematic View

- Light strikes a smooth interface between two transparent materials, e.g. **air and glass**.
- Segments of plane waves can be represented by bundles of rays.
- We often draw only **one ray** for each beam.

---

## Reflection

- Light striking a surface may be **reflected**, **transmitted**, or **absorbed**.
- Reflected light leaves the surface at the **same angle** it was incident on the surface.
- **Important:** angles are measured relative to the **surface normal**.

### Reflection: Specular or Diffuse

- The **smoothness of the interface** between the media determines the nature of reflection and refraction.
- We mainly work with **specular reflection**.

## Index of Refraction

- The interface between two media is characterized by the direction of the **normal** on the interface.
- Optical properties of a material are specified using the **index of refraction** `n` (or **refractive index**).
- The index of refraction is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum `c` and the speed of light in the material `v`:

**n = c / v**

---

## What About the Wavelength?

- The speed of light in vacuum is approximately:
  **c = 3 * 10^8 m/s**
- The index of refraction of a material is defined by:

**n = c / v**

- The speed of light changes when it passes from one medium to another, but the **frequency does not**.
- With **v = λf**, the wavelength changes:

**v = c / n -> λₙ = λ / n**

---

## Refraction

Light travels in a straight line except when:

- it is **reflected**
- it moves from one medium to another

**Refraction**-the bending of light rays when light moves from one medium to a different one-takes place because light travels with different speeds in different media.

- Reference: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction>

## Discussion

### Why Is Glass (`n ~= 1.5`) Transparent?

**Answer:** The bandgap energy **E\_g** for glass is quite large. Photons of visible light do not have enough energy to excite electrons into higher energy levels. Therefore, they do not get absorbed and the glass is transparent.

### Why Does Light Travel Slower Through Glass (`n ~= 1.5`)?

**Answer:** Light (an electromagnetic wave) interacts in a subtle way with the electrons in the crystal lattice of the medium. This creates oscillations throughout the lattice, the net effect of which (through superposition) is that the light is effectively slowed down.

## Reflection and Refraction

### Laws of Reflection and Refraction: Definitions

- Angles of **incidence**, **reflection**, and **refraction** are specified with respect to the **normal** of the interface.
- Assume **monochromatic** (single-color) light.

### Three Laws of Reflection and Refraction: Snell's Laws

1. All rays of **incidence**, **reflection**, and **refraction** lie in **one plane**.
2. The angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence:
   **θᵣ = θₐ**
3. The angle of refraction satisfies:
   **nₐ sin θₐ = n_b sin θ_b**

---

## Snell's Laws: Some Historical Facts

- **Willebrord Snell** (Dutch scientist, 1591-1626) derived his law in **1621**.
- The law was first accurately described by **Ibn Sahl** (Persian scientist) at the Baghdad court in **984**, for lens shaping.

- Reference: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snell's_law>

## Quiz: Refraction

Light passes from medium with index of refraction **nₐ** into a second medium with index of refraction **n_b**. The angles of incidence and refraction are **θₐ** and **θ_b**, respectively.

If **nₐ < n_b**, then...

- (a) **θₐ > θ_b** and the light speeds up as it enters the second medium.
- (b) **θₐ > θ_b** and the light slows down as it enters the second medium. <- !!
- (c) **θₐ < θ_b** and the light speeds up as it enters the second medium.
- (d) **θₐ < θ_b** and the light slows down as it enters the second medium.
- (e) none of the above are true.

## Huygens's Principle

### Huygens Principle

- Every point of a wave front is the source of **secondary wavelets**.
- Each wavelet spreads out in **all directions**.
- The wavelets spread with a speed equal to the **propagation speed of the wave**.

### Huygens's Principle - Reflection

- Wavelets strike the surface:
  - change direction of travel
  - remain travelling at the same speed **v**

### Huygens's Principle - Reflection (continued)

#### Secondary Wave Front

- A line connects points with the same arrival time.
- New source points are created along the surface.

#### Incident Rays Versus Reflected Rays

- Compare the **angle of incidence** with the **angle of reflection**.
- Consider angles in the red triangle.

### Huygens's Principle - Reflection: Conclusion

**Triangle ΔAOP ≅ ΔOAQ -> θᵣ = θₐ**

### Huygens's Principle - Refraction

- Wavelets striking the interface continue at a **different speed** due to medium properties.
- Relations:

**sin θₐ = vₐ t / AO**
**sin θ_b = v_b t / AO**

- This results in **Snell's law of refraction**.

## Why the Sun Becomes Flattened at Sunset

### Refraction

- Light refracts at the **atmospheric interface**.
- Curvature of the interface causes a **varying normal** to the interface.
- The angle of refraction differs for every incident ray.
- This results in flattening of the **bottom side** of the sun.

## Total Internal Reflection

### Total Internal Reflection

- When **n_b < nₐ**, there is a critical angle of incidence **θ_crit** such that **θ_b = 90 deg**.
- From Snell's law:

**sin θ_crit = n_b / nₐ**

- For **θₐ > θ_crit**, all rays are **totally reflected**.

### Total Internal Reflection (continued)

- Ray incident normal to the surface: reflection and transmission
- Increasing angle of incidence: more... more...
- Critical angle reached: transmission along the boundary
- Total internal reflection

### Jewelry

The brilliance of diamonds is to a large extent due to their very high index of refraction:

**n = 2.417**

This gives a correspondingly small **θ_crit**.

- Light entering a cut diamond is totally internally reflected from facets at its back surface and emerges at the front.
- Diamond cutting aims to **maximize reflection**.
- A brilliant diamond cut must maximize light return through the top.
